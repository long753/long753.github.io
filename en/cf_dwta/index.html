CF-DWTA Model: Damage Probability Calculation for Weapon-Target Assignment Considering Cascading Failures

0. Overview

This paper addresses the issue of traditional weapon-target assignment (WTA) models neglecting cascading failure effects by proposing the CF-DWTA model and EPL-AT strategy. The main contributions include:

  1. Constructing a CF-DWTA model that incorporates cascading failure effects and temporal evolution, demonstrating its high applicability to dense formation scenarios.
  2. Developing the EPL-AT strategy, which significantly reduces the computational complexity of damage probability calculations while strictly maintaining high accuracy.

Note: Since the paper is currently under review, detailed simulation data cannot be publicly disclosed at this time. However, the experimental results fully validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

1. Research Background

The traditional Weapon-Target Assignment (WTA) problem aims to maximize combat effectiveness by optimizing allocation strategies under the constraints of limited defense resources. Current research enhances the practical applicability and decision-making value of WTA models through diverse designs of optimization objectives and constraints, such as maximizing expected damage effectiveness and minimizing resource consumption. However, traditional static WTA models neglect the temporal dimension, exhibiting severe myopia in dynamic battlefield environments.

To address this issue, Dynamic Weapon-Target Assignment (DWTA) models have been developed to incorporate temporal dynamics and adapt to changing battlefield environments. While DWTA models account for temporal dynamics, they ignore the spatial damage propagation and sympathetic detonation effects within dense formations, leading to significant deviations in calculating threat destruction probabilities. Although cascading failure effects are increasingly recognized in dense formation modeling, existing methods struggle to balance speed and accuracy. Monte Carlo (MC) simulations offer high accuracy but incur prohibitive computational costs for real-time decision-making; probabilistic graphical models (e.g., Noisy-OR and Loopy Belief Propagation) provide superior efficiency but fail to isolate redundant probabilities within feedback loops, causing severe overestimation biases in cyclic graphs.

To tackle these problems, this paper proposes a Cascading Failure-Dynamic Weapon-Target Assignment (CF-DWTA) model and designs an Effective Path Length Adaptive Truncation (EPL-AT) strategy to efficiently calculate the damage probability of incoming targets.

2.2 Basic Assumptions

This paper establishes the following basic assumptions:

  1. All incoming targets within a single operational stage are assumed to be hit simultaneously.
  2. The post-interception status of any given target is strictly modeled as a binary state (damaged or undamaged).
  3. The interception events executed by individual defensive weapons are considered to be statistically independent.

3. CF-DWTA Model

In dense formation scenarios, the destruction of an incoming target by a defensive weapon can trigger sympathetic detonations, which may cause damage to neighboring targets and lead to cascading failures. Therefore, in the CF-DWTA model, incoming targets are subject to both direct damage from defensive weapons and indirect damage from neighboring targets.

3.1 Damage Probability Composition

The damage probability of an incoming target consists of two parts: the Direct Damage Probability (DDP) caused by defensive weapons, and the Indirect Damage Probability (IDP) caused by adjacent incoming targets. Therefore, the calculation formula for the total damage probability of incoming target jjj is as follows:

pj=1−(1−pjDDP)⋅(1−pjIDP)p_j = 1 - (1 - p_j^{\text{DDP}}) \cdot (1 - p_j^{\text{IDP}})pj​=1−(1−pjDDP​)⋅(1−pjIDP​)

where pjDDPp_j^{\text{DDP}}pjDDP​ represents the direct damage probability of target jjj, and pjIDPp_j^{\text{IDP}}pjIDP​ represents the indirect damage probability of target jjj.

3.2 Direct Damage Probability Calculation

Since the attack events of each defensive weapon are independent, the DDP can be calculated using the standard Noisy-OR model:

pjDDP=1−∏i=1m(1−pijwea)p_j^{\text{DDP}} = 1 - \prod_{i=1}^{m} (1 - p_{ij}^{\text{wea}})pjDDP​=1−i=1∏m​(1−pijwea​)

where mmm is the number of defensive weapons, and pijweap_{ij}^{\text{wea}}pijwea​ is the direct damage probability of defensive weapon iii against incoming target jjj.

3.3 Indirect Damage Probability Calculation

In traditional damage probability calculation models, the indirect damage probability pjIDPp_j^{\text{IDP}}pjIDP​ is often simplified to the standard Noisy-OR model:

pjIDP=1−∏k=1k≠jn(1−mkjcas⋅pk)p_j^{\text{IDP}} = 1 - \prod_{\substack{k = 1 \\ k \ne j}}^{n} (1 - m_{kj}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_k)pjIDP​=1−k=1k=j​∏n​(1−mkjcas​⋅pk​)

where mkjcasm_{kj}^{\text{cas}}mkjcas​ is the indirect damage probability of target kkk against target jjj, and pkp_kpk​ is the total damage probability of target kkk.

Since the above equation fails to isolate the cyclic damage contributions from target jjj itself, directly employing the marginal probability pkp_kpk​ will lead to an overestimation of pjIDPp_j^{\text{IDP}}pjIDP​. To eliminate this phenomenon, this paper introduces the concept of “cavity probability” from statistical physics. It modifies the pkp_kpk​ in the equation to a cavity probability pkH−{j}p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}}pkH−{j}​, whose physical meaning is: the damage probability of target kkk after removing target jjj from the current incoming target set H\mathcal{H}H. Substituting the modified equation yields:

pjH=1−(1−pjDDP)⋅∏k=1k≠jn(1−mkjcas⋅pkH−{j})p_j^{\mathcal{H}} = 1 - (1 - p_j^{\text{DDP}}) \cdot \prod_{\substack{k = 1 \\ k \ne j}}^{n}(1 - m_{kj}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}})pjH​=1−(1−pjDDP​)⋅k=1k=j​∏n​(1−mkjcas​⋅pkH−{j}​)

where pjHp_j^{\mathcal{H}}pjH​ represents the total damage probability of target jjj, and pkH−{j}p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}}pkH−{j}​ represents the cavity probability of target kkk.

3.4 Recursive Equation System

To precisely solve for pkH−{j}p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}}pkH−{j}​, it is necessary to continue tracking and eliminating deeper causal propagation paths, thereby forming the following recursive equation system:

{pjH=1−(1−pjDDP)∏k=1k≠jn(1−mkjcas⋅pkH−{j})pkH−{j}=1−(1−pkDDP)∏a=1a≠j,kn(1−makcas⋅paH−{j,k})paH−{j,k}=1−(1−paDDP)∏b=1b≠j,k,an(1−mbacas⋅pbH−{j,k,a})⋮pr{r,n}=1−(1−prDDP)(1−mrncas⋅pn{n})pn{n}=pnDDP \left\{ \begin{aligned} p_j^{\mathcal{H}} &= 1 - (1-p_j^{\text{DDP}}) \prod_{\substack{k = 1 \\ k \ne j}}^{n} (1-m_{kj}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}}) \\[1ex] p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}} &= 1 - (1-p_k^{\text{DDP}}) \prod_{\substack{a = 1 \\ a \ne j,k}}^{n} (1-m_{ak}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_a^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k\}}) \\[1ex] p_a^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k\}} &= 1 - (1-p_a^{\text{DDP}}) \prod_{\substack{b = 1 \\ b \ne j,k,a}}^{n} (1-m_{ba}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_b^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k,a\}}) \\[1ex] &\quad \vdots \\[1ex] p_r^{\{r,n\}} &= 1 - (1-p_r^{\text{DDP}}) (1-m_{rn}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_n^{\{n\}}) \\[1ex] p_n^{\{n\}} &= p_n^{\text{DDP}} \end{aligned} \right. ⎩⎨⎧​pjH​pkH−{j}​paH−{j,k}​pr{r,n}​pn{n}​​=1−(1−pjDDP​)k=1k=j​∏n​(1−mkjcas​⋅pkH−{j}​)=1−(1−pkDDP​)a=1a=j,k​∏n​(1−makcas​⋅paH−{j,k}​)=1−(1−paDDP​)b=1b=j,k,a​∏n​(1−mbacas​⋅pbH−{j,k,a}​)⋮=1−(1−prDDP​)(1−mrncas​⋅pn{n}​)=pnDDP​​

The final damage probability of each incoming target can be accurately solved by iteratively calculating the equation system above. However, the computational complexity of this equation system is O(n!)\mathcal{O}(n!)O(n!), and the computational cost increases factorially with the problem scale. Therefore, it is necessary to design a simplified calculation method to improve computational efficiency.

4. EPL-AT Strategy

4.1 Strategy Motivation

In real combat environments, the impact of cascading failures attenuates as the propagation path length increases, and cascading effects with longer propagation paths can usually be ignored. Moreover, since a single incoming target only has an indirect damage effect on its neighboring targets, the cascading failure adjacency matrix Mcas\mathbf{M}_{\text{cas}}Mcas​ exhibits sparsity characteristics. Based on the above features, the equation system can be approximately solved by limiting the maximum propagation path length, thereby reducing computational complexity.

4.2 Strategy Principle

Taking the 2nd-order truncation as an example, which means a maximum of two cascading impacts are considered with the propagation path of j→k→aj \rightarrow k \rightarrow aj→k→a. Therefore, the equation system can be approximated as:

{pjH=1−(1−pjDDP)∏k=1k≠jn(1−mkjcas⋅pkH−{j})pkH−{j}=1−(1−pkDDP)∏a=1a≠j,kn(1−makcas⋅paH−{j,k})paH−{j,k}≈1−(1−paDDP)∏b=1b≠j,k,an(1−mbacas⋅pbH−{j,k}) \left\{ \begin{aligned} p_j^{\mathcal{H}} &= 1 - (1-p_j^{\text{DDP}}) \prod_{\substack{k = 1 \\ k \ne j}}^{n} (1-m_{kj}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}}) \\[1ex] p_k^{\mathcal{H}-\{j\}} &= 1 - (1-p_k^{\text{DDP}}) \prod_{\substack{a = 1 \\ a \ne j,k}}^{n} (1-m_{ak}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_a^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k\}}) \\[1ex] p_a^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k\}} &\approx 1 - (1-p_a^{\text{DDP}}) \prod_{\substack{b = 1 \\ b \ne j,k,a}}^{n} (1-m_{ba}^{\text{cas}} \cdot p_b^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k\}}) \end{aligned} \right. ⎩⎨⎧​pjH​pkH−{j}​paH−{j,k}​​=1−(1−pjDDP​)k=1k=j​∏n​(1−mkjcas​⋅pkH−{j}​)=1−(1−pkDDP​)a=1a=j,k​∏n​(1−makcas​⋅paH−{j,k}​)≈1−(1−paDDP​)b=1b=j,k,a​∏n​(1−mbacas​⋅pbH−{j,k}​)​

This strategy automatically disregards cascading propagation effects beyond three nodes by approximating the deep cavity probability pbH−{j,k,a}p_b^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k,a\}}pbH−{j,k,a}​ as pbH−{j,k}p_b^{\mathcal{H}-\{j,k\}}pbH−{j,k}​. Since the degree of cascading failure propagation effects varies among different targets, applying a high-order truncation across all targets would lead to computational redundancy. Therefore, adaptively selecting the truncation order for different targets is an important issue.

To balance computational complexity with computational accuracy, we propose the EPL-AT strategy. This strategy utilizes the cascading failure adjacency matrix Mcas\mathbf{M}_{\text{cas}}Mcas​ to calculate the Effective Maximum Cascading Failure Path Length (EMCF-PL) between different targets, automatically selecting the truncation order for each target.

4.3 Detailed Calculation Process

The detailed calculation process of the EPL-AT strategy is as follows:

  1. Set basic parameters, including the EMCF-PL threshold OmaxO_{\text{max}}Omax​ and probability truncation threshold τ\tauτ.
  2. Search for cascading failure paths between targets using the breadth-first search (BFS) algorithm, with stop conditions: the propagation probability of the path falls below τ\tauτ, the path length exceeds OmaxO_{\text{max}}Omax​, or the target is reached.
  3. Set the truncation order based on the length of the longest cascading failure propagation path.
  4. Repeat the above process until the truncation orders of all targets are determined.
  5. Adjust the calculation model according to the truncation order and compute the damage probabilities.

Algorithm: EPL-AT Strategy

Input: 
    - Direct damage probability pDDp
    - Adjacency matrix Mcas
    - Probability threshold τ
    - Max truncation order Omax
    - Iteration error ε

Output: Final comprehensive damage probability pH

1: Initialize truncation order matrix O ← I
2: FOR each target j ∈ {1, ..., n} DO
3:     Pj ← BFS(Mcas, j, Omax, τ)  // Extract valid cascading paths
4:     FOR each path p ∈ Pj ending at target k DO
5:         O[j,k] ← max(O[j,k], length(p))  // Update truncation order
6:     END FOR
7: END FOR

8: Initialize cavity probability matrix C ← pDDp
9: REPEAT
10:    Cprev ← C  // Store previous cavity probabilities
11:    FOR each target k and cavity node j ≠ k DO
12:        msg_ak ← Compute truncated message using Cprev and O
13:        C[k,j] ← 1 - (1 - pDDp[k]) × ∏a (1 - Mcas[a,k] × msg_ak)
14:    END FOR
15: UNTIL max|C - Cprev| < ε

16: FOR j = 1 TO n DO
17:    pH[j] ← 1 - (1 - pDDp[j]) × ∏k (1 - Mcas[k,j] × C[k,j])
18: END FOR

19: RETURN pH

4.4 Computational Complexity Analysis

Assuming the number of incoming targets is nnn, the logarithmic average truncation order is hhh, the average number of adjacent targets is DDD, and the average number of iterations is III, the computational complexity of the EPL-AT strategy mainly consists of two parts: the complexity of BFS search and the complexity of iterative calculation. For BFS search, it is necessary to traverse all the truncated paths between targets, and the complexity is O(n⋅Dh)\mathcal{O}(n \cdot D^h)O(n⋅Dh); for iterative calculation, each iteration requires updating the cavity probability of nnn targets, and each target needs to consider its adjacent targets, so the complexity is O(I⋅n2⋅Dh)\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^2 \cdot D^h)O(I⋅n2⋅Dh). Therefore, the total complexity of the EPL-AT strategy is O(I⋅n2⋅Dh)\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^2 \cdot D^h)O(I⋅n2⋅Dh), which significantly reduces the computational cost compared to the exact algorithm’s O(n!)\mathcal{O}(n!)O(n!).
